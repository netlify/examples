# Keel-billed Toucan

The Keel-billed Toucan is perhaps Costa Rica's most recognizable bird, featuring one of nature's most spectacular bills. This rainbow-colored appendage combines shades of green, orange, red, and yellow, making the species instantly identifiable. Also known as the Rainbow-billed Toucan, this charismatic bird serves as a flagship species for tropical forest conservation throughout Central America.

## Physical Characteristics

Keel-billed Toucans measure 42-55 cm in length, with their massive bill accounting for nearly one-third of their total body length. Despite its impressive size, the bill is remarkably lightweight, composed of a honeycomb structure of bony struts covered by keratin. The bill's vibrant coloration includes green with an orange stripe along the sides, red tip, and blue lower mandible.

The body is predominantly black with bright yellow on the throat and chest, bordered by a narrow red band. The undertail coverts are vivid red, visible when the bird is in flight. Eyes are surrounded by bright green bare skin, and the legs and feet are blue. Males and females appear identical, though males average slightly larger.

## Habitat and Distribution

In Costa Rica, Keel-billed Toucans inhabit humid lowland forests and forest edges on both Caribbean and Pacific slopes, from sea level to about 1,500 meters elevation. They show remarkable adaptability, thriving in:

- Primary rainforest canopy
- Secondary forest and mature plantations
- Forest edges and clearings with scattered large trees
- Gallery forests along rivers

## Social Behavior and Vocalizations

Keel-billed Toucans are highly social birds, typically seen in small flocks of 6-12 individuals. These groups roost communally in tree cavities, with multiple birds cramming into a single hole. To fit, they fold their tails forward over their backs and tuck their bills under their wings.

Their vocalizations are distinctive and far-carrying. The primary call sounds like a repetitive, croaking "kee-yo" or frog-like "reeep," delivered in slow series. They also produce rattling and clicking sounds. These calls are most frequent during early morning and evening hours.

## Diet and Foraging Behavior

Primarily frugivorous, Keel-billed Toucans consume a wide variety of tropical fruits. They use their long bill to reach fruits at the tips of branches too thin to support their weight. After plucking a fruit, they toss it in the air, catch it in their throat, and swallow it whole.

Their diet includes:

- Fruits from numerous tree species, especially figs, palms, and tropical almonds
- Small reptiles and amphibians
- Large insects
- Bird eggs and nestlings (opportunistically)
- Small mammals (rarely)

Toucans play a crucial role as seed dispersers. They swallow fruits whole and later regurgitate or defecate the seeds at considerable distances from the parent tree, contributing significantly to forest regeneration.

## Breeding and Nesting

Breeding season typically occurs from March through June, though timing varies by location. Keel-billed Toucans are cavity nesters, utilizing natural tree holes or abandoned woodpecker cavities high in the canopy. They do not excavate their own cavities.

Both parents share incubation duties for the clutch of 2-4 white eggs. Incubation lasts approximately 16-20 days. Chicks hatch naked and blind, developing feathers after several weeks. The nestling period extends about 6-7 weeks, with both parents feeding the young.

Young toucans remain with their parents for several weeks after fledging, and families may join larger flocks. Toucans can be aggressive in defending nest sites from other cavity-nesting species.

## The Remarkable Bill

The toucan's enormous bill has fascinated scientists and naturalists for centuries. Recent research has revealed several functions:

- **Thermoregulation**: The bill contains a network of blood vessels that help regulate body temperature by dissipating excess heat
- **Feeding**: Enables reaching fruits on thin branches and intimidating smaller birds at fruiting trees
- **Display**: Used in courtship rituals and social interactions within flocks
- **Defense**: Though not particularly strong, appears formidable to potential predators

The bill's bright colors may serve as recognition signals between individuals and in attracting mates.

## Conservation Status

The Keel-billed Toucan is listed as Least Concern globally, with stable populations throughout most of its range. However, the species faces localized threats:

- Deforestation reducing suitable nesting habitat
- Capture for the pet trade (though less common now)
- Habitat fragmentation isolating populations

In Costa Rica, strong legal protections and extensive protected areas have helped maintain healthy populations. The species adapts reasonably well to secondary forests and can persist in fragmented landscapes if some large trees remain.

## Cultural Significance

The Keel-billed Toucan holds special cultural significance as Belize's national bird. Throughout Central America, toucans feature prominently in indigenous mythology and modern conservation campaigns. Their charismatic appearance makes them ideal ambassadors for rainforest conservation, appearing in countless ecotourism materials and educational programs.

## Observation Tips

Keel-billed Toucans are relatively easy to observe in appropriate habitat. Look for them:

- In fruiting trees, especially figs
- Flying across forest gaps in distinctive undulating flight
- Perched high in canopy trees during midday
- Most active early morning and late afternoon

Their loud calls often reveal their presence before visual confirmation. Patient observation at productive fruiting trees frequently rewards viewers with extended views of feeding and social behavior.

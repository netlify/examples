# Scarlet-thighed Dacnis

The Scarlet-thighed Dacnis is a small, jewel-like tanager endemic to Costa Rica and western Panama. Males display stunning turquoise-blue plumage set off by vivid scarlet thigh patches, creating one of the most spectacular color combinations in Costa Rica's avifauna. This diminutive endemic represents the country's remarkable bird diversity and the importance of protecting lowland and foothill forests that harbor specialized species with restricted ranges.

## Physical Characteristics

Scarlet-thighed Dacnis are tiny birds measuring approximately 12 cm in total length and weighing 11-14 grams. The species exhibits striking sexual dimorphism typical of many tanagers.

Adult males are breathtaking, with brilliant turquoise-blue covering most of the head, back, throat, and breast. The wings and tail are black, creating sharp contrast with the blue body. The most distinctive feature is the vivid scarlet-red thigh patches, visible when birds are perched or in flight. A black mask extends through the eyes, and the crown shows slightly deeper blue than the body.

Females are entirely different, displaying yellow-green plumage throughout with paler underparts. They lack any blue or scarlet coloration, making them easily overlooked. The bill is thin, pointed, and black in both sexes.

Juveniles resemble females but may show duller, more olive tones. Young males gradually acquire adult plumage over several months, passing through intermediate stages with patchy blue and green.

The male's spectacular coloration makes identification straightforward, while females require careful observation and often identification by association with males.

## Habitat and Distribution

As an endemic species, Scarlet-thighed Dacnis have a restricted distribution limited to humid lowland and foothill forests of Costa Rica and western Panama. In Costa Rica, they occur on both Caribbean and southern Pacific slopes from sea level to approximately 1,200 meters elevation.

Preferred habitat includes:

- Humid lowland rainforest
- Foothill forests
- Forest edges and clearings
- Second growth with good canopy development
- Plantations with substantial tree cover (coffee, cacao)

The species requires forest or well-wooded habitat but tolerates moderate disturbance better than many forest specialists. They can persist in agricultural mosaics retaining forest patches and tree cover.

Key locations include Caribbean lowlands around Sarapiquí, Tortuguero region, and southern Pacific areas including Osa Peninsula. The species occurs throughout appropriate habitat within its range but is nowhere abundant.

## Behavior and Mixed-Species Flocks

Scarlet-thighed Dacnis are active, acrobatic birds typically encountered in pairs or small groups. They frequently join mixed-species foraging flocks, associating with other tanagers, honeycreepers, and small insectivorous birds.

Participation in mixed flocks provides several benefits:

- Enhanced predator detection through multiple vigilant individuals
- Information about food resources
- Reduced individual predation risk through dilution effect

Within flocks, dacnis often forage at different heights or on different food types than other species, reducing direct competition. Their small size and agility allow access to thin branches and terminal leaves unavailable to larger birds.

Movement through forest is rapid and energetic, with birds constantly shifting positions while foraging. The brilliant male plumage flashes through foliage, occasionally visible before the tiny bird moves to another location.

## Vocalizations

Scarlet-thighed Dacnis produce thin, high-pitched calls and songs typical of small tanagers. The primary vocalization consists of a series of squeaky notes, sometimes described as insect-like in quality.

Songs are relatively simple, lacking the complexity of larger tanagers. Males sing during breeding season, though the songs are easily overlooked amid the cacophony of forest sounds.

Call notes serve contact functions, maintaining pair bonds and flock cohesion. These thin calls can be difficult to distinguish from other small birds without experience.

The high frequency vocalizations may be adapted to reduce eavesdropping by predators, as high-frequency sounds attenuate rapidly in dense vegetation and are difficult for many predators to localize.

## Diet and Foraging Behavior

Scarlet-thighed Dacnis are omnivorous, consuming both fruit and arthropods with seasonal variation in proportions. The diet includes:

- **Fruits**: Small berries, mistletoe fruits, and various forest fruits
- **Nectar**: Occasionally visit flowers for nectar
- **Insects**: Small insects, spiders, and other arthropods
- **Larvae**: Caterpillars and other soft-bodied invertebrate larvae

Foraging occurs primarily in the canopy and subcanopy, with birds working through terminal foliage and flower clusters. Their small size and agility allow exploitation of resources at thin branch tips.

The thin, pointed bill is well-suited for probing flowers, gleaning small insects from leaves, and handling small fruits. Birds may hang upside-down or adopt other acrobatic positions while foraging.

Frugivory makes them seed dispersers for various plant species, contributing to forest regeneration. The combination of fruits, nectar, and insects provides dietary flexibility allowing year-round residence in forest habitats.

## Breeding Biology

Breeding season appears to span much of the year with peak activity during early wet season (April-July). Scarlet-thighed Dacnis are monogamous, with pairs maintaining territories during breeding.

Nests are small open cups constructed from fine plant fibers, rootlets, and moss. They are typically placed in shrubs or small trees at low to moderate heights, 2-8 meters above ground. The nest exterior is often camouflaged with moss and lichen.

The female lays 2 eggs, white to pale blue with dark markings. Details of incubation and nestling periods remain incompletely documented for this species, but likely follow patterns typical of small tanagers (incubation approximately 12-14 days, nestling period 12-15 days).

Both parents participate in nest building and feeding nestlings. Family groups may remain together for some time after fledging, with juveniles learning foraging techniques from adults.

## Endemic Species Conservation

Being endemic to a relatively small geographic area makes Scarlet-thighed Dacnis inherently vulnerable to threats affecting their limited range. The species is currently listed as Least Concern, but faces ongoing pressures:

- Deforestation throughout lowland Costa Rica and Panama
- Forest fragmentation isolating populations
- Agricultural intensification reducing habitat quality
- Climate change potentially affecting suitable habitat distribution

In Costa Rica, protected areas on Caribbean and southern Pacific slopes provide important strongholds. Extensive forest protection in areas like Osa Peninsula ensures habitat security for significant populations.

The species' tolerance of secondary forest and agricultural landscapes with tree cover provides some resilience to habitat modification. However, complete deforestation eliminates populations.

As an endemic, Scarlet-thighed Dacnis have high conservation priority despite current stable status. Range-restricted species warrant special attention in conservation planning.

## Observation Opportunities

Finding Scarlet-thighed Dacnis requires visiting appropriate lowland or foothill forest habitat and often considerable patience. The species' small size, rapid movements, and tendency to remain in canopy foliage make detection challenging.

Best observation strategies include:

- Joining mixed-species foraging flocks
- Checking flowering and fruiting trees
- Early morning when birds are most active
- Locations with good canopy access (observation towers, elevated trails)
- Caribbean lowland lodges with established bird feeders and viewing areas

Males are conspicuous when visible due to brilliant plumage, but often appear only briefly before moving to new foraging locations. Females are easily overlooked and often detected only when associating with males.

Birding lodges in Sarapiquí and other appropriate areas sometimes attract dacnis to fruiting plants and hummingbird feeders, providing excellent viewing opportunities.

For birders visiting Costa Rica, successfully finding this gorgeous endemic represents a highlight. The species exemplifies the rewards awaiting those who explore forests patiently and carefully.
